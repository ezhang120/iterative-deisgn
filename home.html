Iterative Design

The purpose of this group project was to design an interface for an emerging startup.
To begin, we sketched out ideas for the interface and made wireframes based on our sketches. Then, we made multiple iterations of a high-fidelity prototype. After receiving feedback on our prototype from our peers as well as an industry guest, we made changes to our prototype and submitted it to a user testing service.

Introduction: Our Startup

Milkrun is an online marketplace that aims to connect city dwellers to local food products by mitigating both the time spent and steps taken for food delivery and distribution. It strives to supply fresh groceries in a timely manner as well as help customers establish real relationships with local farms. Taking a user-centric approach, we decided to design a website that allows users who are looking for more organic options to purchase groceries from local stores and subscribe to a grocery-delivery service.

Wireframes

After discussing our sketches, we made wireframes for our interface based on our favorite designs.

  • Home: We wanted to display important news at the top of the page and have modules for location and subscriptions because they are the two most important features of the site.
  • Product: Information on the product should be clearly listed when the user clicks on it, along with contact information for each producer so users can easily connect with local stores.
  • Profile: The profile page is a generic, simple page where users can edit their location as well.
  • Our Stores: Users should be able to find a store that they would like to find specific products from, and they should be able to filter the stores by type of store and distance. We used radio buttons instead of a drop down so that all choices are visible.
  • Shop: All products should have an appealing image and have a button to easily increase or decrease quantity underneath. The products should be filterable by several different categories for an easy find. The search bar at the top should be a free search so users can find products through a variety of key words.
  • Location: The modal window is minimal so viewers can simply enter their location and select a suggested address with no confusion.
  • Shopping Cart: Items are listed clearly on the left with options to remove or update quantity. A clear summation of price is displayed on the left, with an option to confirm the purchase after reviewing the list of items.
  • Checkout: A simple credit card form is displayed on the right and a simple order summary is displayed on the right so users can review their purchase and quickly checkout.

Studio Critique:

During our studio, we received some critiques regarding our hi-fi mockup. Below, we have included a list of the critique feedback.

  1. Having both “Our Stores” and “Shop” tabs in the navigation bar is confusing because the selection of stores mainly acts as a filter when users are shopping. Create a hierarchy of functionalities and build other functionalities off of that.
  2. The “Update” button in the “Shopping Cart” page isn’t necessary because it requires an extra click from users.
  3. The Roberto Monospace font gives the company a technological aesthetic. Think about how typography conveys a message to users.
  4. The +/- button in the “Shop” page is not consistent with the “Update/Remove” button in the “Shopping Cart” page.
  5. Show item price in the “Shopping Cart” page. Don’t expect the users to remember the prices of each item.
  6. The “Home” page doesn’t look scrollable because the “Find Your City” box and the “Subscribe” button aren’t very visible when the “Home” page first loads.
  7. It’s unnecessary to have to fill out new information on the checkout page when it should already be saved through the profile.

High Fidelity Mockup Version 2:

  1. We removed the “Our Stores” tab in the navigation bar to maintain a consistent hierarchy of functionalities. Users can now navigate to the "Shop" page if they wish to see the entire selection of stores by clicking on the “Browse Stores” link under the “Producer” filter on the “Shop” page.
  2. We removed the “Update” button on the checkout page to update the number of items in the cart because we decided it would be best if the prices and items are automatically updated without the need for an extra update button.
  3. We changed our website font to one that has less of a technological feel.
  4. We replaced the +/- button on the “Shop” page to an input box (similar to the one on the “Checkout" page) for more consistency.
  5. We added the prices for individual items on the “Checkout" page.
  6. We moved the “Find Your City” and “Subscribe” boxes up (so the tops of the boxes can be seen) to give an indication that there is more to be seen at the bottom of the homepage.
  7. We changed the "Checkout" page so information is automatically filled and the user only needs to confirm that the information is correct or make necessary changes.

User Testing:

We used UserTesting.com, a remote user testing service, to test our revised high fidelity prototype. We received responses from 3 users, with each user representing a different demographic and socioeconomic group.

The users were given the following main task:
Imagine that you are purchasing groceries for the week. The task is to navigate through the demo and reach the checkout page. [Disclaimer: This is an interactive mockup made on a prototyping software and is not affiliated with the actual company.]

In addition, they were also provided with 3 subtasks to complete:

  1. Without leaving the homepage, describe what you would select first. **Do not select anything yet.** If you would leave without clicking anywhere, please explain why.
  2. (Do this for each page) Without leaving this page, in your own words, describe what do you think you can do on this website? Be specific.
  3. Navigate your way to the Select Location page. Comment on your experience.

At the conclusion of the study, each user completed four follow-up questions.

  1. What frustrated you most about this site?
  2. If you had a magic wand, how would you improve this site?
  3. What did you like about the site?
  4. Who do you think would use this site and why?

User Testing Results & Analysis:

Contrary to our expectations, users found navigating our prototype confusing and difficult. A few specific problems brought to our attention were:

  • Users did not understand what exactly the company and service entailed and were confused on the details of the subscription and location UI elements.
  • When navigating to the “Select Location” page, users commented how it wasn’t clear how the search bar worked and what the dropdown menu options were.
  • The UI for increasing the number of items in the cart was vague, and the “Profile” section was left untouched.
  • The design of the “Find Location” modal window was confusing because it looked very similar to the design of the homepage, which peeked through at the bottom.

Overall, users thought the simplicity of the website was nice but lacked key information and visual indicators. User confusion was further amplified by mistakes we made in setting up the user testing. Due to our inexperience with the user testing website, we made the subtasks separate from the main task with the assumption that the main task would be clearly visible, when that was not the case. This led to an abnormally high focus on the “Select Location” page and lack of critique on the “Checkout” page. In the future, we’d like to structure the user testing so that the subtasks and the main task are much more directly related.

Potential Interface Changes:

Based on the user testing videos, here are some potential changes we can make to our interface.

  • Remove the “Select Location” and “Subscription” module from the home page and replace it with a short blurb with information about the company and the service.
  • Specify what kind of location users should enter into the search bar (they should be entering in their personal address).
  • Add a “Select Location” module to the top navigation bar and show suggestions of user-inputted location in the dropdown menu when the user is searching for their location.
  • Modify item descriptions in the “Shop” page to be clearer on the exact quantity of a product being sold and for how much.
  • Remove the ability to navigate to the “Shop” page by clicking on the homepage banner. Add a small button to navigate to the advertised sale instead of making the entire banner responsive.
  • Replace the “Select Location” modal window with an entirely new page for clarity (or change the size of the modal window so it takes up the entire page and doesn’t show the design underneath).

Conclusion:

Through this Iterative Design project, I learned what it is like to work on a UI/UX project as a team. I also had the opportunity to receive and give design critiques, which helped me to be more open to different design choices. After finishing this project, we contacted Milkrun and shared our final prototype, and we are currently waiting for their response.
